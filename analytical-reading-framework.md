# A Framework for Analytical Reading
*A reusable lens for examining any serious news article or report — one piece at a time, in your own voice, under your own name.*

---

## How to Use This

Work through the phases in order the first few times; later, they'll blend into a single habit of mind. Not every question applies to every article. The goal is not to complete a checklist but to know, when you finish reading, exactly what you believe, why you believe it, and how confident you are entitled to be.

A useful discipline: answer Phase 1 and 2 questions *before* forming any opinion about whether the piece is good or its conclusions true. Description first, evaluation second. Most sloppy analysis comes from skipping straight to judgment.

---

## Phase 1 — The Objective and the Frame
*What is this piece trying to do, and how has it framed the world?*

1. What question is this article actually answering? State it in one sentence. Is that the question the headline implies?
2. Who produced this, for what audience, under what incentives (commercial, institutional, ideological, deadline-driven)?
3. What is treated as the "problem" here — and who defined it as a problem? Would other stakeholders define it differently?
4. What time frame and geographic scope has the author chosen? What does that choice include and exclude?
5. What genre is this — reporting, analysis, opinion, investigation, summary of someone else's work? Am I holding it to the right standard for its genre?

## Phase 2 — Criteria and Definitions
*By what standard is anything here judged a success, a failure, a crisis, or a trend?*

6. What are the key terms ("surge," "reform," "efficiency," "extremist," "record high"), and are they defined — or doing quiet argumentative work undefined?
7. When the piece says something is working or failing, what measure of success is implied? Who chose that measure?
8. Are numbers given with the denominators, baselines, and time comparisons needed to interpret them? (A "40% increase" from what, since when, out of how many?)
9. Is the unit of analysis consistent — people, dollars, incidents, percentages — or does it shift when convenient?

## Phase 3 — The Evidence
*What actually supports each load-bearing claim?*

10. What are the three most important factual claims in this piece? For each: what is the source — a document, a dataset, a named expert, an anonymous official, another news outlet?
11. How many independent sources support the central claim? Are the "multiple sources" truly independent, or do they trace back to one origin?
12. Where does the evidence come from firsthand observation, and where from someone's characterization of something the reporter never saw?
13. What is the strongest single piece of evidence here? What is the weakest claim the argument still depends on?
14. Is any evidence quoted selectively? Can I check the underlying document, study, transcript, or dataset myself — and did the piece link to it?
15. What would count as evidence *against* the article's thesis, and did the author look for it?

## Phase 4 — The Causal Story
*What mechanism is being claimed, explicitly or by arrangement of facts?*

16. What cause-and-effect relationship does the piece assert or imply? Say the mechanism out loud: "X led to Y because ___."
17. Is the claimed cause merely correlated with the effect? What else changed at the same time?
18. What alternative explanations exist for the same facts, and does the piece acknowledge or test them?
19. Does the causal story require people or institutions to behave in ways that are plausible given their actual incentives and constraints?
20. If the causal claim is true, what else should be true in the world? Do I observe those downstream consequences?

## Phase 5 — Transparency and Method
*How was this made, and can I audit it?*

21. Does the piece distinguish clearly between what is known, what is inferred, and what is speculated?
22. Are corrections, uncertainties, and limitations acknowledged — or is confidence uniform from start to finish (a warning sign)?
23. Who was given a chance to respond, and how is their response handled — fairly summarized or perfunctorily included?
24. What can I verify in fifteen minutes? (Pick one checkable claim and actually check it.)

## Phase 6 — The Unasked
*What is absent, and is the absence meaningful?*

25. Whose perspective is missing entirely — and would including it change the story?
26. What obvious follow-up question does the piece never ask? Why might that be?
27. What is the base rate? Is this event actually unusual, or made to feel unusual by attention alone?
28. If the opposite finding had emerged, would this outlet have published it as prominently?

## Phase 7 — Synthesis and Your Own Ledger
*What do you now believe, and what do you owe your readers?*

29. In one paragraph, in my own words: what did I learn, what remains uncertain, and what would change my mind?
30. What do I need to disclose to my readers — my priors, my sources, the limits of what I checked — so they can audit *me* the way I audited the article?

## Design Principles (So You Can Extend This Yourself)

**Separate description from evaluation.** The early phases force you to state what the piece *is* before judging what it's *worth*. Any new question you add should declare which mode it belongs to.

**Every claim has a provenance.** The framework repeatedly asks not "is this true?" but "how would anyone know?" Chains of attribution matter more than confidence of tone. Extend the framework by asking provenance questions of any new claim type you encounter — images, leaked documents, model outputs, statistics.

**Falsifiability as a habit.** Questions 15, 20, 28, and 29 all ask what would disconfirm a claim. A framework that only gathers supporting considerations is a machine for confirming what you already thought. When you add questions, make sure at least some of them could embarrass you.

**Audit the frame, not just the facts.** Individual facts can all be true while the selection and arrangement misleads. That's why the framework opens with objective and frame and closes with the unasked — accuracy and honesty are different properties.

**Symmetry between reader and writer.** Every standard you apply to the article eventually applies to your own analysis (Phase 7). This keeps the exercise honest and keeps your published work auditable. Any question worth asking of the New York Times is worth asking of your own draft.

**Proportion, always.** Confidence should scale with evidence; prominence should scale with importance. Many failures of analysis are not falsehoods but distortions of proportion.
